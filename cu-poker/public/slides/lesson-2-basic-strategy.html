Basic Strategy

Hand selection, position, aggression, and sizing — the four ideas that make you a winning beginner.

↓ use arrow keys or spacebar

Why beginners lose

Three leaks cost everything

Playing too many hands

If you play 50% of hands, you're entering most pots with trash. Trash loses money over time.

Passive play

Checking and calling lets opponents realize their equity for free. Bets build pots — and fold equity.

Ignoring position

Playing the same hand the same way from every seat is a million-dollar mistake.

Fix these three and you'll beat most home games without opening a book.

Idea 1 of 4

Play fewer, better hands

Premium — always playable

AA · KK · QQ · JJ · AK

These hands dominate random holdings. Raise them every time.

Strong — playable from most seats

TT · 99 · AQ · AJs · KQs

Solid but need to be handled with a little care out of position.

Speculative — late position only

Suited connectors · Small pairs · Suited aces

Big upside (straights, flushes, sets) but only when cheap and in position.

Trash — fold it

J4 · 92 · 73 · K3o · Q5o

Unsuited, disconnected, not premium. Folding is a free plus-EV action.

Check your understanding
You're under the gun (first to act preflop). Which of these hands is the clearest fold?
Correct. J4 offsuit is trash — disconnected, unsuited, dominated by any real hand. Fold from any position, especially UTG.
AKs is premium. Always raise.
Pocket nines are a solid raising hand. Not close to a fold.
AQs is a strong hand — play it.

Out of position (OOP)

You act before your opponent on every street. You show information first. Harder.

In position (IP)

You act after your opponent. You see their action first. Easier. More profitable.

Rule of thumb: play more hands from late position, fewer from early position.

Idea 3 of 4

Bet, don't call

When you have a decision, raising and betting are usually better than calling or checking. Two reasons:

Value

If your hand is good, betting gets more chips in the pot from worse hands.

Fold equity

Even when your hand is weak, a bet can win the pot right now if your opponent folds. Calling never folds anyone out.

Calling stations — players who call too much — are the easiest opponents to beat. Don't be one.

Idea 4 of 4

Bet sizing, simplified

Preflop open

2.5–3× BB

Standard opening size. Bigger from early position, smaller from the button.

Continuation bet

33–50% pot

Small is fine when the board is dry. Go bigger on wet boards with draws.

Value bet (river)

66–100% pot

When you're confident you're ahead, charge them.

Red flag: a very small bet screams "please call." A very big bet on the river usually means the nuts or a bluff.

Check your understanding
You open-raise preflop and one player calls. The flop comes K♦ 7♣ 2♠ — a dry, disconnected board. You have A♠ K♠ (top pair, top kicker). Best continuation bet sizing?
Right. On a dry, disconnected board there are few draws to charge. A small c-bet (33%) gets value from worse Kings and can fold out middle pairs cheaply. Bigger sizes price yourself out.
Checking lets your opponent realize equity for free and wastes your range advantage. Bet.
Overbetting a dry board mostly folds out worse hands and inflates the pot against better ones.
Shoving for 50× the pot is a classic beginner overbet. Doesn't get called by worse.
Leaks to avoid

Beginner mistake checklist

  • Limping — calling the big blind preflop. Either raise or fold.
  • Slow-playing monsters — set, straight, flush — bet them. You'll get called more than you think.
  • Calling "just to see" — you need a reason. "Curiosity" isn't one.
  • Chasing with bad odds — covered in Lesson 3. Drawing needs math.
  • Playing when tilted — losing a hand badly doesn't make the next hand a better call.
  • Not paying attention — who raised? From where? Information is free at the table.
Check your understanding
You open-raise from UTG with A♠ A♥. The button 3-bets you. The small blind cold 4-bets all-in for 200 big blinds. The button folds. What's the worst play?
Right. Folding Aces preflop against a single opponent is almost always a disaster — you're ahead of literally every other hand. This is a snap call (or re-raise).
Aces are the best preflop hand. Against one opponent, you should never fold them preflop — they're favored against every other starting hand in the deck.
Recap

Four ideas, in order

  1. Play fewer, better hands. Fold trash. Raise premiums.
  2. Use position. Play more from the button, less from UTG.
  3. Be the bettor. Raising and betting beat calling. Calling never folds anyone out.
  4. Size with purpose. Dry boards: small. Wet boards: bigger. River value: bigger still.
